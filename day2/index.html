Section 1
Section I — Foundations of Machine Learning

Before any AWS service or specific algorithm, you need a clean mental map of what machine learning actually is, what kinds of problems it solves, and how a machine learning project flows from raw data to a deployed model. The four topics in this section give you that map. Read them in order. Every later topic in the guide assumes you have these four firmly in your head.

Topic 2
Topic 1 — The Mental Model You Need Before Anything Else
4-Step Exam Decision Ladder

Before we discuss a single algorithm or AWS service, I want to plant one idea in your head, because every question on this exam is some flavor of it. Whenever you read a scenario on the exam, you are really being asked to walk down a four-step ladder. The first step is to identify what kind of machine learning the situation calls for, and there are essentially four families to choose from. The second step is to decide what shape the output needs to take, which is what we call the problem type. The third step is to pick the right AWS service to do the work, and the fourth step is to decide how that service will deliver its predictions to the user. Master this ladder and the exam stops being a memorization contest. Every scenario is a guided walk down those four rungs.

Think of the exam writer as someone who has hidden the answer behind a small story. The story might describe a hospital wanting to detect tumors in X-rays, or a streaming service wanting to suggest movies, or a factory floor wanting to spot defective products on a conveyor belt. Each of these stories will hint at all four rungs of the ladder, and your job is to read those hints. With practice, the answers fall out almost automatically.

Topic 3
Topic 2 — The Four Types of Machine Learning

Machine learning is the field of teaching computers to find patterns in data without being explicitly programmed for every situation. The way we teach the computer, however, depends entirely on what kind of data we have, and this is what gives us four distinct flavors of machine learning.

The first and most common flavor is called supervised learning, and the easiest way to understand it is to think about how a child learns to recognize a cat. You show the child many photos and you say "this is a cat," and you also show them photos that are not cats and say "this is not a cat." The child learns by example, but only because each example came with a correct answer attached to it. In supervised learning, the data we give the model is exactly like this. Every training example has both an input, such as the photo, and a label, such as the word "cat" or "not cat." The model studies thousands or millions of these labeled pairs and learns the relationship between the inputs and the outputs. Once trained, the model can take a new input it has never seen before and predict what the label should be. Loan approval, fraud detection, house price prediction, and tumor diagnosis are all classic supervised learning problems because each one has historical examples where the correct answer is already known. Whenever an exam question mentions historical data with known outcomes, or labels, or training examples that include the right answer, the question is pointing you at supervised learning.

The second flavor is unsupervised learning, and here the situation is different. Imagine the same child, but this time you simply hand them a thousand photos with no labels at all and you tell them to organize the pile however they want. The child might naturally group the photos by what they show, putting all the animal photos in one stack, all the building photos in another, and so on, even though no one told them what categories to use. Unsupervised learning works in this same way. The data has no labels, and the model's job is to discover hidden structure on its own. This structure usually takes one of two forms. It might be a set of clusters, where the model groups similar items together, which is exactly what happens when a retailer wants to discover natural buying segments among its customers without deciding the segments in advance. Or the structure might be the detection of items that do not fit, which is how unsupervised learning becomes useful for finding fraud, anomalies, and outliers in datasets where almost everything is normal. When an exam question describes data that has no predefined categories, or asks you to discover patterns, or talks about segmenting customers without telling you what the segments should be, the question is pointing at unsupervised learning.

The third flavor is reinforcement learning, and this is the one most students find unusual at first because it does not look like the other two. Instead of learning from labeled data or unlabeled data, the model learns by doing. Picture training a dog. The dog tries something, perhaps sitting when you say "sit," and you respond with either a treat or a gentle correction. Over many repetitions, the dog learns which actions lead to rewards. In reinforcement learning, the model is called an agent, and it operates inside an environment. At each moment, the agent takes an action, and the environment responds with either a positive reward or a penalty. The agent's goal is to learn a policy, which is just a fancy word for a strategy, that earns the most reward over time. This kind of learning is everywhere in robotics, in game-playing AI like the systems that beat humans at chess and Go, in autonomous driving simulations, and in dynamic pricing systems where an algorithm constantly adjusts prices and observes how sales respond. When you see words like agent, environment, reward, policy, or trial and error in an exam question, you are looking at reinforcement learning.

The fourth flavor, semi-supervised learning, is the practical compromise between supervised and unsupervised learning. In the real world, labeling data is often expensive and slow. A radiologist labeling X-rays is using highly paid time. A linguist labeling speech recordings can only do so much in a day. So organizations often end up with a small pile of carefully labeled data and a much larger pile of unlabeled data sitting unused. Semi-supervised learning is a way to use both piles together. The unlabeled data helps the model learn the general shape of the world, while the small labeled pile teaches the model what the names for things are. When an exam question mentions limited labeled data alongside a large unlabeled dataset, or talks about reducing labeling costs, semi-supervised learning is the answer.

A small mental exercise before we move on. Consider this scenario: a robot vacuum learns the optimal cleaning path by trying different routes and being rewarded for the percentage of floor it covers. Which type of learning is this? The agent and reward language is the giveaway. This is reinforcement learning. Now consider another: a bank wants to flag suspicious transactions and has five years of transactions, each marked as fraud or legitimate. The presence of labels makes this supervised learning. One more: a retailer with fifty million customer records wants to find natural shopping segments without deciding the segments in advance. No labels, looking for hidden groupings. This is unsupervised learning. If those three felt easy, the foundation is in place.

Topic 4
Topic 3 — The Five Problem Types

The type of machine learning tells us about the data we have. The problem type tells us about the shape of the answer we want. These are different questions, and a single type of learning can address several different problem types.

The first problem type is classification, which is the task of predicting a category from a finite set of choices. The output is what we call discrete, meaning it is one of a small number of possibilities. When a spam filter decides whether an email is spam or not spam, that is binary classification because there are exactly two outcomes. When an image recognition system decides whether a photo shows a cat, a dog, or a bird, that is multi-class classification because there are three options. And when a content tagging system decides that a single image shows both a beach and a sunset and people, that is multi-label classification because more than one label can apply to the same input. The unifying idea is that the answer is always a label from a fixed menu. Whenever an exam question asks which class something belongs to, or whether a yes-or-no decision needs to be made, classification is the problem type.

The second problem type is regression, which is the task of predicting a continuous number. The output is not a category but a value on a numeric scale, like a house price of four hundred and twenty-seven thousand dollars, or a forecast revenue of one point two million, or a predicted energy demand of four hundred and fifty megawatt-hours. The crucial distinction between regression and classification is the nature of the answer. The question "Will this customer churn?" is classification because the answer is yes or no. But the question "How many days until this customer churns?" is regression because the answer is a number along a continuum. When an exam scenario asks you to forecast, predict a price, or estimate a quantity, you are looking at a regression problem.

The third problem type is clustering, which is the unsupervised counterpart to classification. Instead of being told the categories, the model finds them. A retailer might want to discover that its customers naturally fall into five buying personas based on their purchase history, without specifying what those personas should be. A research team might want to organize a million news articles into themes that emerge from the data itself. In all these cases, no labels exist, and the algorithm groups items by similarity. When you see phrases like grouping similar items, segmenting customers without predefined labels, or finding natural groupings, clustering is the answer.

The fourth problem type is anomaly detection, which is the search for the unusual. Most of the time, the system is looking at data that follows a normal pattern, and only rarely does something strange happen. Detecting unusual login attempts that come from a different country at three in the morning, finding fraudulent credit card transactions among millions of legitimate ones, or spotting a vibration signature on a factory machine that suggests imminent failure are all anomaly detection problems. What makes this different from regular classification is that the rare event is, well, rare, which means you usually do not have many examples of it. So even though anomaly detection sometimes uses supervised techniques, it often leans on unsupervised methods that learn what normal looks like and then flag whatever deviates from that. Words like outlier, abnormal, deviation from normal, rare event, and unusual spike all point to anomaly detection.

The fifth problem type is recommendation, which is the art of suggesting things people will like. Netflix recommending movies, Amazon suggesting products you might also buy, Spotify generating Discover Weekly playlists, and news sites surfacing articles tailored to your reading history are all recommendation problems. The model learns from the behavior of many users and uses that combined wisdom to predict what each individual user will enjoy. When you see personalized recommendations, suggestions of similar items, or the classic "users who bought X also bought Y" pattern, recommendation is the problem type.

Topic 5
Topic 4 — The Machine Learning Pipeline
7-Stage ML Pipeline

Every real machine learning system, no matter how simple or sophisticated, moves through the same seven stages. Understanding this pipeline matters not just for the exam but because each AWS feature you will memorize maps to one of these stages. Once you can place a service on the pipeline, you understand what it is for.

The first stage is data collection, which is exactly what it sounds like. You gather raw material from databases, log files, image archives, audio recordings, sensor readings, clickstream data from websites, and any other source relevant to the problem. This stage is not glamorous, but it is the foundation of everything that follows. There is a saying in machine learning that captures this perfectly: garbage in, garbage out. A model trained on biased, incomplete, or wrong data will give you biased, incomplete, or wrong predictions, and no amount of clever algorithm work can rescue it. The exam loves to test this idea, so when a scenario describes a model that produces unfair or biased outcomes, the correct answer is almost always to fix the data, not to fix the algorithm.

The second stage is preprocessing, where you take the raw, messy data you collected and prepare it for use. Real-world data is never clean. There are duplicate rows, missing values, fields in inconsistent formats, and noise that has nothing to do with the underlying pattern. Preprocessing involves removing duplicates so the same record does not unfairly influence training, handling missing values by either filling them with a sensible estimate or dropping the row, normalizing numeric fields so that a salary in dollars and a height in centimeters do not overwhelm each other due to scale differences, converting categorical fields into numbers a model can use, and stripping out the noise. Without preprocessing, even the best algorithm will struggle to learn anything useful.

The third stage is feature engineering, which is where human insight does much of the heavy lifting. A feature is an input variable that the model uses to make predictions, and feature engineering is the craft of creating useful features from raw data. The raw data might be a date of birth, but the useful feature is the person's age. The raw data might be a transaction timestamp, but the useful feature is the hour of the day, the day of the week, or whether it was a weekend. The raw data might be an address, but the useful features might be the city, the postal code, and whether the location is in a metropolitan area. Old-school machine learning, like the popular XGBoost algorithm, leans heavily on good feature engineering. Modern deep learning often learns features automatically from raw data, which is one reason it is so powerful for images and text. The exam tests this distinction, so remember that traditional ML rewards careful feature engineering while deep learning is more forgiving.

The fourth stage is training, which is where the model actually learns. You take your prepared data and feed it to an algorithm, which adjusts its internal numbers, called parameters, in a way that reduces the error between its predictions and the actual labels. The algorithm has a loss function, which is a measure of how wrong its predictions are right now, and an optimizer, which is the method it uses to nudge the parameters in the right direction. There are also hyperparameters, which are the settings you choose before training begins, such as how fast the model learns and how large each batch of training examples should be. Training is computationally expensive and time-consuming, often requiring specialized hardware like GPUs.

The fifth stage is evaluation, where you test the trained model on data it has never seen during training. This is critical because a model can become very good at memorizing the training data without actually learning the underlying pattern. By holding back some data and using it only for evaluation, you get an honest estimate of how well the model will perform in the real world. The metrics you use depend on the problem type, and the choice of metric is so important that we will spend an entire section on it later.

The sixth stage is deployment, where you put the model into production so that real users or applications can actually use it. Deployment can take several forms. Sometimes you need a real-time endpoint that responds to each request within milliseconds, like a fraud check during a credit card transaction. Sometimes you need batch processing that scores millions of records overnight, like a churn prediction system that runs once a day. Sometimes you need asynchronous processing for very large inputs that take minutes to handle, like analyzing a long video. And sometimes the model lives on a device itself, in what is called edge deployment, like a phone running a face-unlock model.

The seventh and final stage is monitoring, which is the ongoing watch you keep over the deployed model. Real-world data changes over time, and a model that worked perfectly when launched can quietly degrade. The most important things to monitor are data drift, which means the input data has shifted away from what the model was trained on, and model drift, which means the predictions are no longer as accurate as they used to be. You also watch for bias creeping into the predictions, for latency problems if the model becomes slow, for cost overruns if the endpoint is using too much compute, and for outright errors. When monitoring detects a problem, you typically retrain the model with fresh data, which sends you back to stage four. This is why machine learning is a loop, not a straight line.

To make these seven stages feel concrete rather than abstract, picture a logistics company building a model to predict whether a package will be delivered late. In data collection, the team gathers three years of historical shipment records from their database, including origin and destination zip codes, package weight, carrier, weather conditions on the shipping day, and whether each shipment was actually late. In preprocessing, they discover that ten percent of the records have missing weather data, that some weight fields are in pounds and others in kilograms, and that there are duplicate records from a database migration; they fix all of this. In feature engineering, they convert raw timestamps into useful features like day of week, month, and whether the shipment crossed a weekend, and they create a derived feature for distance from the origin and destination zip codes. In training, they feed the cleaned data to an XGBoost classifier and let it learn which combinations of features predict lateness. In evaluation, they hold back the most recent six months of data and check how often the model correctly predicts the lateness of those shipments it has never seen, finding an F1 score of 0.78 which they consider acceptable. In deployment, they place the model behind a real-time endpoint so that the website can predict lateness during checkout for every new shipment. In monitoring, they watch for the day when accuracy starts dropping, which finally happens nine months later when a new carrier joins the network and the model has never seen that carrier before; the team retrains with the new data and the loop continues. Every AWS service you will study in later topics fits somewhere on this same seven-stage map.

Section II — Data, Parameters, and Inference

Once you understand what machine learning is, the next layer of fluency is understanding the materials and machinery that make a model work. This section covers the data you train on, the numbers the model learns versus the numbers you set yourself, and the difference between the learning phase and the prediction phase. These distinctions are the source of many exam traps, and getting them right makes the rest of the guide much easier.

Topic 7
Topic 5 — Training Data Concepts

Now that you understand the pipeline at a high level, let us go deeper into the data side, because this is where many real projects succeed or fail.

The first concept is labeling, which is the act of attaching the correct answer to each training example. For supervised learning, this is non-negotiable. If you want a model to recognize cats, someone has to look at thousands of photos and write down which ones are cats. Labeling can be done by humans, by other models, or by some combination of the two, and AWS provides a service called SageMaker Ground Truth specifically to coordinate this work. Ground Truth allows you to set up labeling workflows where human annotators do the work, but it also uses machine learning to reduce the human effort. As the model gets better at the task, it starts auto-labeling the easy cases and saves the humans for the hard ones, a technique called active learning.

The second concept is data splits. When you train a model, you should never use all your data for training. Instead, you split the data into three parts. The training set, which is usually around seventy to eighty percent of the total, is what the model actually learns from. The validation set, around ten to fifteen percent, is used to tune hyperparameters and pick the best model from several candidates. The test set, the remaining ten to fifteen percent, is held back completely and used only at the very end to give you an honest estimate of how the model will perform on truly new data. The cardinal rule, which the exam will test in disguised forms, is that you must never train on the test set and never tune on the test set. The moment you peek at the test set during development, your final accuracy number is no longer trustworthy.

The third concept is class imbalance, which is one of the most common real-world problems and a frequent exam topic. Imagine a fraud detection dataset where ninety-nine percent of transactions are legitimate and only one percent are fraudulent. A naive model that always predicts "legitimate" will be ninety-nine percent accurate, which sounds impressive until you realize it is completely useless for catching fraud. There are several ways to deal with class imbalance. You can oversample the minority class by duplicating or synthesizing more fraud examples. You can undersample the majority class by dropping some legitimate examples. You can use a more sophisticated technique called SMOTE that generates synthetic minority samples by interpolating between real ones. You can assign class weights, which tells the model that mistakes on the rare class cost more. And you can change your evaluation metric away from accuracy and toward something like F1 score or recall, which we will discuss soon. The exam frequently presents imbalanced scenarios and asks you to choose the right metric or technique.

The fourth concept is data augmentation, which is the practice of artificially expanding your training data by creating modified versions of existing examples. For images, you can rotate them, crop them, flip them horizontally, change their brightness, add noise, or zoom in on parts of them. For text, you can replace words with synonyms or translate to another language and back, which is called back-translation. For audio, you can stretch the time, shift the pitch, or add background noise. The purpose of all this is twofold. It expands your effective training set without you having to collect more data, and it teaches the model to be robust to variations it might encounter in the real world. Augmentation is one of the most reliable ways to reduce overfitting.

Topic 8
Topic 6 — Parameters and Hyperparameters

This distinction is small but important, and the exam tests it directly. The difference comes down to who sets the value: the model itself, or you.

A parameter is a number that the model learns automatically during training. The classic example is the weights in a neural network. When training begins, these weights start as random numbers, and as the model sees more and more training examples, the optimizer adjusts the weights so that the model's predictions get closer to the correct answers. By the end of training, the weights have settled into values that capture the patterns in the data. You never set these values by hand. They emerge from the training process. Other examples of parameters include the biases in a neural network, the learned word embeddings in a language model, and the split points in a decision tree.

A hyperparameter is a number that you choose before or during training. Hyperparameters control how the training itself happens. The learning rate, which determines how big each adjustment to the parameters is, is a hyperparameter you must pick. The batch size, which determines how many training examples are processed at once, is another. The number of epochs, which determines how many times the model passes through the entire training set, is another. For neural networks, the number of layers and the number of neurons per layer are hyperparameters. For tree-based models, the number of trees and the maximum tree depth are hyperparameters. For regularization, the strength of the penalty is a hyperparameter.

The challenge with hyperparameters is that you do not know in advance which values will work best. Choosing them is part art and part systematic search, and the search process itself has a name: hyperparameter tuning, sometimes called hyperparameter optimization. AWS SageMaker has a feature called automatic model tuning that searches the hyperparameter space for you, often using a clever method called Bayesian optimization that learns from the results of previous attempts to choose smarter values for the next attempt.

A simple way to remember the distinction: parameters are the model's homework that it does itself, while hyperparameters are the rules of the homework that you set.

To make this concrete, imagine a team building a fraud detection model on credit card transactions. Before training begins, they make a series of hyperparameter choices. They set the learning rate to 0.001, because in past experiments a higher rate caused the model to bounce around without converging. They set the batch size to 256 transactions, because that fits comfortably in their GPU memory. They set the maximum tree depth to 6 for their XGBoost model, because deeper trees overfitted. They also set regularization strength to 0.1 to control complexity. None of these numbers are learned from the data; the team picks them. Then training begins, and now the model learns its parameters automatically. For each input feature like transaction amount, hour of day, merchant category, and distance from the cardholder's home, the model learns a weight that captures how strongly that feature pushes the prediction toward "fraud." It learns thousands of split points across its trees. None of those weights or split points are set by humans; they emerge from the training process. Two weeks later, when the model accuracy is disappointing, the team does not retrain on different data. Instead, they launch a SageMaker automatic model tuning job that systematically tries different hyperparameter combinations using Bayesian optimization, and discovers that a learning rate of 0.0005 and a tree depth of 8 actually works better. Hyperparameters are knobs you turn; parameters are what the model becomes once you turn the knobs and start it running.

Topic 9
Topic 7 — Training Versus Inference

Once you understand parameters and hyperparameters, the difference between training and inference falls into place naturally. These are the two phases in the life of a model, and they have very different characteristics.

Training is when the model learns. During training, the model starts with random or default parameters and processes the training data many times, gradually adjusting its parameters to reduce its prediction errors. Training is computationally heavy and typically requires specialized hardware like GPUs. It can take hours, days, or even weeks, depending on the size of the model and the dataset. Training is also expensive, both in terms of compute cost and the human effort required to set it up correctly. Crucially, training is something you do periodically, not continuously. You train once, deploy the model, and then retrain only when needed.

Inference is when the trained model makes predictions on new data. During inference, the model's parameters are frozen, and it simply applies the learned patterns to the new input to produce an output. Inference is computationally lighter than training, although it can still be expensive at scale because you might need to handle thousands of requests per second. The latency requirements for inference are usually much stricter than for training, because users expect quick responses. Inference happens continuously throughout the life of the deployed model.

The way you deliver inference matters enormously, and AWS gives you four ways to do it. Real-time inference is for situations where you need an answer immediately, within milliseconds, such as a fraud check during a payment, a chatbot replying to a user, or a recommendation appearing on a website as it loads. Batch inference, which AWS calls Batch Transform, is for situations where you need to score a huge dataset offline and there is no rush, such as scoring all your customers overnight or generating monthly churn predictions. Asynchronous inference is for situations where the input is very large or the processing takes minutes, such as analyzing a video file or processing a long document, and the user is willing to wait for the result. Serverless inference is for situations with unpredictable, sporadic traffic where you do not want to pay for servers sitting idle.

The exam will give you scenarios and ask which inference mode fits. The key signals are response time requirements, payload size, and traffic patterns. A scenario describing a website that needs sub-second responses points to real-time. A scenario describing a nightly job that processes millions of records points to batch. A scenario describing video processing that takes ten minutes per file points to async. A scenario describing an internal tool used a few times a day points to serverless.

Section III — Model Quality and Evaluation

A model that runs is not the same as a model that works. This section teaches you how to recognise when a model is failing in subtle ways, how to describe those failures using the precise language of bias and variance, and how to choose the right evaluation metric for the situation. Half the trick of the exam lives in this section, because almost every scenario question rests on whether the candidate knows when accuracy lies, when recall matters, and when a high training score is actually a warning sign.

Topic 11
Topic 8 — Overfitting and Underfitting

We are now ready to discuss the single most important concept in machine learning. If you understand overfitting and underfitting deeply, you will understand half the field.

Overfitting happens when a model learns the training data too well, including all its quirks and noise, and as a result fails to generalize to new data. Imagine a student who memorizes every question and answer from a stack of practice exams. When you give them the practice exams again, they get every answer right. But when you give them a real exam with new questions on the same material, they fail miserably because they never actually learned the underlying ideas. They just memorized the surface patterns. An overfit model behaves the same way. It might score ninety-nine percent on training data and only sixty-five percent on test data. The large gap between these two numbers is the telltale sign of overfitting.

Overfitting has several common causes. The model might be too complex, having more parameters than the data can really support. There might not be enough training data, making it easy for the model to memorize rather than generalize. The training might have run for too many epochs, allowing the model to start fitting noise after it had already learned the real patterns. There might be too many features, some of which are just noise that the model latches onto. Whatever the cause, the cure involves making the model less able to memorize and more forced to generalize. You can collect more training data, which makes memorization harder. You can apply regularization, which is a mathematical penalty for being overly complex. You can reduce the model's complexity directly by using fewer parameters or simpler architectures. You can use dropout, a technique specifically for neural networks that randomly turns off some neurons during training so the network cannot rely too heavily on any single pathway. You can use early stopping, which means watching the validation accuracy during training and stopping the moment it stops improving, before the model starts to memorize. You can use cross-validation to get more reliable estimates of how well the model is really doing. And you can use data augmentation to artificially expand the training set with variations.

Underfitting is the opposite problem and is much easier to diagnose. An underfit model is too simple to capture the pattern in the data, and as a result it performs poorly on both the training data and the test data. Continuing the student analogy, this is the student who studied only the chapter titles. They could not pass the practice exam, and they cannot pass the real exam either, because they never learned anything substantive. An underfit model might score sixty percent on training and fifty-eight percent on test. Both numbers are low, and the gap between them is small.

Underfitting usually comes from using a model that is too simple for the problem, from having too few features to capture the relationships, from stopping training too early, or from choosing the wrong algorithm for the task. The cure involves making the model more capable. You can use a more complex model, add more or better features, train for longer, or reduce regularization if it is being too aggressive.

A small mental exercise. Suppose you have a model with ninety-eight percent training accuracy and seventy-one percent validation accuracy. What is happening? The huge gap tells you the model is memorizing the training data while failing to generalize, which is overfitting. The fixes are more data, regularization, dropout, simpler model, or early stopping, any of which the exam might list as the correct answer. Now suppose another model has fifty-five percent on both training and validation. The low scores everywhere tell you the model is too simple to learn the pattern, which is underfitting. The fix is a more complex model or better features.

Topic 12
Topic 9 — Bias and Variance

Bias and variance are deeper, more mathematical ways of thinking about overfitting and underfitting. Understanding them gives you the language to discuss model errors precisely, and the exam uses this language directly.

Bias in this context refers to the error introduced by overly simple assumptions in the model. If you try to fit a straight line to data that is actually shaped like a curve, the line will systematically miss the pattern no matter how much data you give it, because a line simply cannot bend. That systematic error is bias. High bias means the model is too rigid and is missing the real structure in the data. High bias is essentially the same condition as underfitting.

Variance refers to the error introduced by the model being too sensitive to the specific training set it saw. A high variance model is like a piece of paper that crumples differently every time you handle it. Slight changes in the training data lead to wildly different models. Imagine a curve so flexible that it passes through every single training point exactly. Now imagine you swap in a slightly different training set. The curve will look completely different, because it was contorting itself to fit the noise as well as the signal. High variance is essentially the same condition as overfitting.

The challenge of machine learning is that bias and variance pull in opposite directions, which is why the topic is called the bias-variance tradeoff. A very simple model has high bias and low variance, because it is too rigid to capture the pattern but at least it is consistent. A very complex model has low bias and high variance, because it can capture the pattern but only by becoming unstable. The art is to find the sweet spot where both bias and variance are low enough that the model both captures the real pattern and stays stable across different training sets.

A useful mnemonic to remember the difference: bias is bad assumptions, the model assumes too simple a shape; variance is volatility, the model swings wildly with each training set.

To make this tangible, imagine three teams trying to predict house prices in a city. The first team uses a simple linear regression model that draws a straight line through average price versus square footage. Their model has high bias because real housing prices depend on many things beyond square footage, and a line cannot capture the curve where prices accelerate at the high end. The model is consistent across different training samples but consistently wrong in the same way. The second team goes to the opposite extreme and uses a deep neural network with millions of parameters trained on only a thousand homes. Their model has high variance because there is not enough data to constrain such a flexible model, so the network ends up memorising quirks of the training set. If they swap in a different sample of a thousand homes, they get a substantially different model. The third team uses a moderately complex model like XGBoost trained on the full dataset; their model captures the real curve in the data without contorting itself around individual points, achieving low bias and low variance simultaneously. The third team wins. The exam tests this trio of cases by describing symptoms — high training error and high test error means high bias, low training error but high test error means high variance, both low means a healthy model — and asking you to diagnose which condition is at play.

Topic 13
Topic 10 — Evaluation Metrics
Precision / Recall / F1 / AUC Decision Chart

Choosing the right metric to evaluate a model is one of the most consequential decisions in machine learning, and the exam tests it relentlessly. The wrong metric can make a useless model look successful or a great model look like a failure. Let us build up the metrics from first principles.

For binary classification, every single prediction the model makes lands in one of four boxes. If the actual answer is positive and the model predicted positive, that is a true positive. If the actual answer is positive but the model predicted negative, that is a false negative, meaning the model missed a real positive case. If the actual answer is negative and the model predicted negative, that is a true negative. And if the actual answer is negative but the model predicted positive, that is a false positive, meaning the model raised a false alarm. These four numbers form what is called a confusion matrix, and every classification metric is built from them.

The simplest metric is accuracy, which is the total number of correct predictions divided by the total number of predictions. Accuracy answers the question, of all the predictions I made, how many did I get right? Accuracy is intuitive and useful when the classes are roughly balanced, meaning each class has a similar number of examples. But accuracy has a famous trap: when the data is imbalanced, accuracy becomes deeply misleading. If ninety-nine percent of credit card transactions are legitimate and only one percent are fraud, a model that predicts "legitimate" for every single transaction will have ninety-nine percent accuracy. That number sounds impressive but the model is utterly useless because it never catches a single fraud case. The exam will trap you here repeatedly, so when you see imbalanced data, your first instinct should be to reach for a different metric.

Precision is a metric that focuses on the quality of positive predictions. It asks the question, of all the things I flagged as positive, how many actually were positive? You compute precision by dividing the number of true positives by the total number of things you flagged as positive, which is true positives plus false positives. Precision is the right metric when false alarms are expensive. The classic example is a spam filter. If a spam filter incorrectly marks an important email from your boss as spam, you might miss it entirely, which is a significant cost. So you want a spam filter with high precision, meaning that when it says something is spam, you can trust that it really is.

Recall, sometimes called sensitivity, focuses on coverage. It asks the question, of all the actual positive cases that exist out there, how many did I catch? You compute recall by dividing the number of true positives by the total number of actual positives, which is true positives plus false negatives. Recall is the right metric when missing a positive case is expensive. The classic example is cancer screening. If a screening test misses a real cancer case, the patient does not get treated and may suffer terrible consequences. So you want high recall, meaning you catch as many real cancer cases as possible, even at the cost of some false alarms that get followed up with further tests.

There is a fundamental tradeoff between precision and recall. You can usually trade one for the other by adjusting the model's decision threshold. If you make the model more eager to predict positive, you will catch more real positives but also raise more false alarms, which means recall goes up and precision goes down. If you make the model more conservative, you will reduce false alarms but also miss some real positives, which means precision goes up and recall goes down. There is no free lunch. You choose where to sit on this tradeoff based on which kind of error is worse in your domain.

The F1 score is the way to balance precision and recall in a single number. F1 is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, computed as two times their product divided by their sum. The harmonic mean has a useful property: it is closer to the smaller of the two numbers, so if either precision or recall is bad, F1 will be bad too. This makes F1 the right metric when both kinds of errors matter and you want a single combined number. F1 is also the standard choice for imbalanced datasets, because it does not get fooled by the class imbalance the way accuracy does.

The AUC-ROC is a different kind of metric. ROC stands for receiver operating characteristic, and it is a curve that shows how the model's true positive rate and false positive rate change as you sweep the decision threshold across all possible values. AUC stands for area under the curve, and it summarizes the ROC curve as a single number between zero and one. An AUC of one means the model perfectly separates positives from negatives at every threshold. An AUC of zero point five means the model is no better than random guessing. An AUC below zero point five is actually worse than random, which means you should flip the predictions. AUC-ROC is the right metric when you want to compare classifiers without committing to a particular threshold, because it captures the model's discrimination ability across all possible operating points.

For regression problems, the metrics are different because the predictions are numbers, not classes. Mean absolute error is the average of the absolute differences between predictions and actual values. Mean squared error is the average of the squared differences, which penalizes large errors more heavily than small ones. Root mean squared error is the square root of mean squared error, which has the nice property of being in the same units as the target variable. R squared, also called the coefficient of determination, measures how much of the variance in the target the model explains, on a scale from zero to one.

The exam will give you scenarios and ask which metric to use. A few patterns to remember. For balanced classes, accuracy is fine. For imbalanced data, use F1 or AUC-ROC. When false positives are expensive, use precision. When false negatives are expensive, use recall. For spam filters, use precision. For medical diagnosis, use recall. For fraud detection on imbalanced data, use F1 or recall. For comparing binary classifiers without a fixed threshold, use AUC-ROC. For regression, use RMSE or MAE.

Neural networks are the engines of modern AI, and deep learning is what powers everything from image recognition to large language models. This short section gives you the conceptual foundation you need: what a neural network actually is, how it learns, and which specific architectures dominate which kinds of problems. The exam tests this material at a conceptual level rather than a mathematical one, so understanding the intuitions is far more important than memorising formulas.

Topic 15
Topic 11 — Neural Network Basics

Neural networks are the engines of modern AI, and although the math underneath can get intricate, the core ideas are intuitive and you only need to understand the ideas for this exam.

A neural network is built from very simple units called neurons, sometimes called nodes. Each neuron takes some numerical inputs, multiplies each input by a weight, adds them all up along with a bias term, runs the result through what is called an activation function, and produces an output. The weights and the bias are the parameters that the model learns during training. The activation function is what makes neural networks powerful, because without it, a neural network would just be doing linear math, no matter how many neurons you stacked together. The activation function adds non-linearity, which is what lets the network learn curves and complex shapes.

Neurons are organized into layers. The input layer receives the raw features, such as the pixel values of an image or the numerical attributes of a customer record. Then come one or more hidden layers, where the real pattern-finding happens. Each hidden layer takes the outputs of the previous layer, applies its own weights and biases and activations, and passes the result on to the next layer. Finally, the output layer produces the prediction. When a network has many hidden layers stacked on top of each other, we call it deep, and that is where the term deep learning comes from.

There are several common activation functions to know. The most popular by far for hidden layers is ReLU, short for rectified linear unit, which is just the function that outputs the input if it is positive and zero otherwise. ReLU is simple, fast, and works remarkably well in practice. For the output layer of a binary classification problem, sigmoid is common, because it squashes any input into a value between zero and one that can be interpreted as a probability. For multi-class classification, softmax is the standard, because it produces a set of probabilities that sum to one across all the possible classes. Tanh is an older activation that you will see in some sequence models.

The mechanism by which a neural network actually learns is called backpropagation, and it is conceptually simple even though the calculus behind it is involved. The network makes a prediction by passing the input through all the layers, which is called the forward pass. The prediction is compared to the correct answer to compute an error. Then the error is propagated backward through the network, layer by layer, and at each layer the algorithm calculates how much each weight contributed to the error. The weights are then nudged in the direction that would have reduced the error. This whole forward-error-backward-update cycle happens for every batch of training examples, thousands or millions of times, and gradually the weights settle into values that make accurate predictions.

Topic 16
Topic 12 — Deep Learning Architectures

Once you understand basic neural networks, you can appreciate why specific architectures dominate specific kinds of problems. Three architectures matter for this exam.

Convolutional neural networks, abbreviated CNN, are the architecture of choice for images and other visual data. The reason they work so well on images is that they exploit a key property of visual information: local patterns matter. A regular neural network would treat each pixel as completely independent of its neighbors, which throws away the spatial structure of the image. A CNN instead uses small filters that slide across the image and detect local patterns like edges, textures, and shapes. These low-level patterns get combined in deeper layers into higher-level concepts like eyes, faces, or whole objects. This hierarchical way of building up from local patterns to complete scenes is exactly how human vision is thought to work, and it is why CNNs are used everywhere visual content matters. Image classification, object detection, facial recognition, medical image analysis, manufacturing defect detection, and the perception systems of self-driving cars are all CNN territory. When the exam mentions images, video, pixels, or visual content, CNN is the architecture being asked about.

Recurrent neural networks, abbreviated RNN, are designed for sequential data, where the order of the information matters. The defining feature of an RNN is that it has a kind of memory: the output at any time step depends not only on the current input but also on the hidden state from the previous time step. This is what lets an RNN process a sentence word by word or a time series day by day, and remember some of what it has already seen. RNNs were the dominant architecture for language and speech for many years, and they are still useful for time series forecasting, sensor data analysis, and any problem where you need to model how patterns evolve over time. However, RNNs have a famous weakness: they struggle with long-range dependencies. By the time an RNN has read a fifty-word sentence, it has largely forgotten the first few words, which makes it hard to capture relationships that span long distances. This weakness is what made the next architecture revolutionary.

Transformers are the architecture behind modern large language models, including GPT, Claude, Llama, and every other model you have heard of in the generative AI space. Transformers solved the long-range dependency problem with a mechanism called attention, which allows every token in a sequence to directly look at every other token. There is no need to march through the sequence step by step like an RNN. Every token has a kind of weighted line of sight to every other token, and the model learns how much attention to pay to each connection. This has two huge consequences. First, transformers can handle very long sequences without forgetting the start. Second, they can be trained in parallel across all positions in the sequence at once, instead of sequentially, which makes training dramatically faster and lets the architecture scale to billions of parameters. Transformers are now used for text generation, translation, summarization, question answering, chatbots, code generation, and basically everything in natural language processing. They are also being adapted to images and other domains. When the exam mentions large language models, foundation models, generative AI, attention, or modern chatbots, transformers are the architecture being referenced.

A simple way to remember the three: CNN for images because they exploit local visual patterns, RNN for sequences because they process step by step, and transformers for language and generative AI because attention scales beautifully.

To anchor this in real scenarios, picture three different companies. A manufacturer of solar panels wants to detect microscopic defects in production-line images; the patterns are spatial — edges, cracks, and texture changes that occur in particular regions of each panel — so a CNN is the natural fit, and indeed the team starts from a pretrained ResNet model and fine-tunes it on their defect images. A weather forecasting startup wants to predict the next hour's rainfall from a sequence of radar snapshots; the data is naturally sequential and the order matters, so an RNN or its more sophisticated variants like LSTM are appropriate, although modern teams increasingly use transformer-based time series models for the long-range memory advantage. A legal-tech company wants to summarise hundred-page contracts and extract specific clause types; the data is text, the relationships span long distances within the document, and the team wants to leverage a pretrained foundation model, so they reach for a transformer-based large language model accessed through Amazon Bedrock. Each architecture matches the shape of the data: CNN for grids, RNN for sequences, transformer for long-range language and beyond.

Section V — Production Machine Learning

A model that lives only in a research notebook is not a useful model. This section covers MLOps, which is the discipline of getting machine learning systems into production and keeping them working as the world around them changes. The exam tests MLOps concepts directly through scenario questions about monitoring, retraining, drift detection, and pipeline automation.

Topic 18
Topic 13 — MLOps

MLOps is the discipline of building and operating machine learning systems reliably, and it borrows heavily from DevOps, which is the same idea applied to regular software. The motivation behind MLOps is that getting a model to work in a research notebook is a small fraction of what it takes to make machine learning useful in a real business. The much harder work is getting the model into production, keeping it running, monitoring it, retraining it as the world changes, and doing all this safely and reproducibly.

A central concept in MLOps is model versioning. Every time you train a new model, you should track exactly which version it is, what data it was trained on, what hyperparameters were used, and what its performance metrics were. This matters for several reasons. If a new model breaks production, you need to be able to roll back to the previous version quickly. If two models are competing in evaluation, you need to compare their performance fairly. If a regulator asks how a particular decision was made six months ago, you need to be able to reproduce the exact model that made it. And if you are working in a regulated industry like banking or healthcare, you may be legally required to maintain this audit trail.

The pipeline that ties all of this together is built on three concepts borrowed and adapted from software engineering. Continuous integration means that whenever someone changes the code, the data pipeline, or the model training script, the system automatically runs tests to make sure nothing is broken. Continuous delivery means that new models are deployed to production safely, often using techniques like canary deployments where the new model handles a small fraction of traffic at first to verify it works before taking over completely. Continuous training is the machine learning addition to this pattern, and it means that the model retrains automatically when triggers fire. Those triggers might be a drop in accuracy, the arrival of new data, the detection of data drift, a change in business rules, a seasonal shift, the discovery of bias, or a regulatory requirement. AWS provides SageMaker Pipelines as the service that orchestrates all of this.

Monitoring in production is the other critical piece of MLOps. Beyond simply checking that the model is still running, you need to monitor several specific things. Accuracy or some business metric tells you whether the model is still useful. Latency tells you whether predictions are still fast enough. Error rates tell you whether requests are failing. Data drift tells you whether the inputs the model is seeing have shifted away from the inputs it was trained on, which is one of the most common reasons models silently degrade. Model drift, sometimes called concept drift, tells you whether the relationship between inputs and outputs has changed, which can happen when the world itself changes, as it did during the early months of the COVID pandemic when shopping patterns shifted overnight. And bias monitoring tells you whether the model is treating different groups fairly, which matters both ethically and legally.

To see why MLOps matters in practice, picture a credit card fraud detection model that worked perfectly when it launched in January. Six months later, the team starts getting complaints that legitimate transactions are being declined. Without MLOps tooling, they have no idea what changed; they cannot tell whether the model itself shifted, whether the data shifted, or whether some upstream system changed. With MLOps tooling, they look at SageMaker Model Monitor and immediately see that data drift was flagged in May when a new payment processor came online and started sending transactions in a slightly different format. They look at SageMaker Pipelines and see that the last retraining run failed two weeks ago because of an upstream data quality issue, so the model has been running on stale weights since then. They look at the model registry and find the previous version, which they can roll back to within minutes while they fix the retraining pipeline. Two days later, the new retraining runs successfully on fresh data, the new model passes its evaluation gates, gets deployed via a canary release to ten percent of traffic for safety, and then to full production. The whole loop took two days because the MLOps infrastructure was already in place. Without it, the same problem would have taken weeks to diagnose and another week to fix.

Topic 15 — SageMaker Training Job Types

When you actually train a model in SageMaker, you have three options that trade off control against effort.

The first option is to use a built-in algorithm, of which AWS provides many. These are ready-made implementations of common algorithms, optimized for SageMaker's infrastructure, and you do not have to write any algorithm code yourself. You just point them at your data, set a few hyperparameters, and start training. For classification problems, the built-in XGBoost is a popular choice because it is one of the most accurate general-purpose algorithms available. For regression, Linear Learner handles many situations well. For clustering, K-Means is the standard. For anomaly detection, Random Cut Forest is purpose-built. For recommendations, Factorization Machines work well. For time series, DeepAR is a sophisticated neural network forecaster. For image classification, there are ready-made architectures based on ResNet and similar networks. The built-in algorithms are the right choice when your problem fits a common pattern and you want fast results without writing custom code.

The second option is script mode, where you bring your own training script written in a popular framework like TensorFlow, PyTorch, or scikit-learn. SageMaker handles the infrastructure, scaling, and managed services, while you control the algorithm and training logic. This is the right choice when you want custom modeling logic but still want SageMaker to handle the heavy lifting of compute provisioning and orchestration.

The third option is to use a custom container, where you bring your own Docker image with whatever code, libraries, and dependencies you want. This gives you full control over everything inside the container, but you also take on responsibility for everything inside it. This is the right choice when you have unusual requirements, like a niche framework, custom dependencies, or proprietary algorithms that do not fit either of the easier options.

The general rule is to use the simplest option that fits your needs. Start with built-in algorithms, move to script mode if you need custom logic, and only reach for custom containers when nothing else will do.

A few concrete scenarios make the choice clearer. A retail analytics team wants to predict next month's sales for each store using two years of historical data; they reach for the built-in DeepAR algorithm because forecasting from time series is exactly what it is built for, and they avoid writing any algorithm code. A research group at a university has developed a novel attention mechanism for medical imaging and wants to train it on chest X-rays; they use script mode with PyTorch because the algorithm is custom but PyTorch handles the framework needs, and SageMaker handles the GPU infrastructure. A pharmaceutical company has a proprietary molecular simulation library written in C++ that needs specific CUDA versions and a specific Python environment; they package everything into a custom Docker container because no other option can satisfy the exotic dependency requirements, and they accept the operational overhead of maintaining the image themselves. The exam tests this same progression: use the simplest fit unless the scenario describes specific reasons that force you up the ladder.

Topic 22
Topic 16 — SageMaker Deployment Types
SageMaker Deployment Type Chooser

We touched on this earlier in the inference discussion, but let me make sure the four deployment types are fully clear, because the exam tests this directly.

A real-time endpoint is a long-running, always-available service that responds to each request within milliseconds. You use real-time endpoints when latency is critical, such as for fraud detection during a payment, a chatbot generating a reply, or a recommendation appearing on a webpage as it loads. Real-time endpoints are continuously running, which means you pay for the compute even when no requests are coming in.

Batch Transform is for offline, large-scale prediction jobs. Instead of running a continuous service, you launch a batch job that processes a large dataset all at once and saves the results. Use batch transform when you need to score many records and have no real-time requirement, such as scoring all your customers overnight, generating monthly churn predictions, or running predictions on a historical dataset for analysis.

Asynchronous inference is the right choice when individual requests have large payloads or take a long time to process, but the user can tolerate some delay. The classic case is video analysis, where a single request might involve a one-gigabyte video file that takes ten minutes to process. With async inference, the request goes into a queue, the model processes it when it can, and the result is delivered when ready.

Serverless inference is for traffic patterns that are sporadic and unpredictable. Instead of paying for an always-on endpoint, serverless inference scales up when requests arrive and scales down to zero when they do not. Use this when you have an internal tool used a few times a day, or any scenario where a long-running endpoint would be wasteful.

The exam pattern is fairly consistent. Phrases like low latency, real-time, during a transaction, or sub-second response point to real-time endpoints. Phrases like overnight, large dataset, no real-time requirement, or process millions of records point to batch transform. Phrases like large payload, long processing time, or asynchronous response point to async inference. Phrases like unpredictable traffic or rarely used point to serverless.

Forecasting predicts future numbers from historical data. Recommendation predicts which items a user will like. Fraud detection and anomaly monitoring predict which observations are unusual. Each of these has a dedicated AWS service.

Amazon Forecast is the time-series forecasting service. You give it historical data along with related variables, and it predicts future values. It is the right choice for demand forecasting, sales forecasting, inventory planning, and workforce demand prediction. Whenever the exam describes predicting future values based on a time-ordered history, Forecast is the answer.

Amazon Personalize is the recommendation engine, and it uses the same underlying technology that powers Amazon's own recommendations on amazon.com. You feed it user interaction data, and it produces personalized recommendations, similar item suggestions, and personalized rankings. When the exam describes recommending products, content, or anything else to users based on their behavior, Personalize is the answer.

Amazon Fraud Detector is the dedicated fraud detection service. It is designed for online payment fraud, fake account detection, and transaction risk scoring. There is an important practical note about this service: as of November 7, 2025, AWS stopped accepting new customers for Fraud Detector, although existing customers continue to use it. For new fraud detection projects, AWS now points customers toward SageMaker. The exam may still mention Fraud Detector, so you should know what it does conceptually, but do not expect questions about deep implementation details.

Amazon Lookout for Metrics detects anomalies in business and operational metrics. It is designed to monitor things like revenue, web traffic, conversion rates, and other key performance indicators, and it alerts you when those metrics behave unexpectedly. When the exam describes detecting unusual changes in business metrics or KPIs, Lookout for Metrics is the answer.

There are two more specialised members of the Lookout family that are worth recognising. Amazon Lookout for Vision detects defects in images of manufactured products, which is the right answer when an exam scenario describes a factory inspecting items on a conveyor belt for visual flaws. Amazon Lookout for Equipment detects unusual patterns in sensor data from industrial machines, which is the right answer when an exam scenario describes predictive maintenance based on vibration, temperature, or other sensor readings.

A common exam trap in this group is the difference between Forecast and Personalize. Forecast predicts numbers over time, like next month's sales or next quarter's demand. Personalize recommends items to specific users, like which movie a particular viewer should watch next. Both involve prediction, but the shape of the answer is completely different. If the question asks for a number, it is Forecast. If the question asks for a ranked list of items tailored to a user, it is Personalize.

Topic 28
Topic 21 — Generative AI Services

The newest category of AWS AI services is built around foundation models, which are large pre-trained models such as Anthropic's Claude, Meta's Llama, and Amazon's Titan family. These models can be used directly for tasks like text generation, summarisation, translation, code generation, question answering, and many others, without any training of your own. AWS exposes them through two distinct services, and the difference between them is one of the most testable distinctions in the entire exam.

Amazon Bedrock is the fully managed service for foundation models. You access models through a single API, and AWS handles all the infrastructure, scaling, and updates. Bedrock is the right answer whenever an exam scenario describes building a generative AI application without managing compute or model hosting. Bedrock includes several useful sub-features that the exam may ask about: Knowledge Bases, which let you implement retrieval-augmented generation where the model answers questions based on your own documents stored in a vector database; Agents, which let you build AI applications that can call APIs and orchestrate multi-step workflows; Guardrails, which let you enforce safety policies on prompts and outputs; and a model evaluation feature for comparing different foundation models on your own benchmarks. There is one important practical detail: when you fine-tune or customise a model in Bedrock, you must use Provisioned Throughput to host it, because the customised model is no longer available through the standard on-demand pricing model.

Amazon Q is a generative AI assistant built on top of foundation models. It comes in two flavours, each aimed at a different audience. Amazon Q Business is the enterprise assistant for non-technical users; it connects to the company's own data sources and answers questions about internal documents, policies, and processes. Amazon Q Developer is the assistant for software engineers; it helps with writing, explaining, and debugging code, and it integrates directly into IDEs.

The clean rule for distinguishing Bedrock from Q is that Bedrock is the platform you build on, while Q is the finished product you consume. If the exam scenario describes a developer building a custom generative AI application that uses foundation models through an API, that is Bedrock. If the scenario describes a business user asking questions about their own company's documents, or a developer using an AI coding assistant inside their IDE, that is Amazon Q.

A related distinction the exam likes to test is Bedrock versus SageMaker JumpStart, both of which expose foundation models. JumpStart sits inside SageMaker and gives you pretrained models that you can fine-tune and deploy on your own SageMaker infrastructure, with full control over the lifecycle. Bedrock gives you a managed API with no infrastructure on your part. If the question emphasises managed API access with no servers to manage, the answer is Bedrock. If the question emphasises fine-tuning and deploying inside SageMaker with full control, the answer is JumpStart.

Topic 31
Topic 23 — The Exam Traps

The exam writers are clever, and they know which mistakes candidates commonly make. The following traps catch most people, so let me walk you through the deeper truths underneath the surface confusion.

The first trap is the accuracy trap. When you see a high accuracy number on imbalanced data, your alarm bells should go off immediately. A model that always predicts the majority class can score ninety-nine percent accuracy while being completely useless. On imbalanced data, the right metrics are F1 score, recall, or AUC-ROC, not accuracy.

The second trap is Rekognition versus Textract. Both deal with images, so candidates often pick wrong. The distinction is what you want from the image. If you want to understand the visual content, the faces, objects, scenes, then Rekognition is correct. If you want to extract the text, tables, or forms inside the document, then Textract is correct. A scanned invoice goes to Textract, not Rekognition.

The third trap is Transcribe versus Polly. These are mirror opposites. Transcribe converts audio to text, while Polly converts text to audio. The mnemonic transcribe writes down, Polly reads aloud captures the difference perfectly.

The fourth trap is Comprehend versus Kendra. Both deal with text, but they do different things. Comprehend analyzes text to determine sentiment, entities, and other linguistic properties. Kendra searches across collections of documents and returns answers to natural language questions. If you are analyzing the meaning of a piece of text, use Comprehend. If you are searching across many documents, use Kendra.

The fifth trap is Forecast versus Personalize. Forecast predicts numbers over time, like next month's sales. Personalize recommends items to users, like which movie to watch next. Both involve predictions, but the shape of the output is completely different.

The sixth trap is SageMaker versus pre-built AI services. The general rule is to use a pre-built service if one fits your problem, because it is cheaper, faster, and requires no ML expertise. Use SageMaker only when you need a custom model on your own data, when you need full control over the ML lifecycle, or when no pre-built service does what you need.

The seventh trap is the inference deployment trap. Real-time, batch, and async are not interchangeable. Real-time is for low-latency requirements, batch is for offline bulk processing, and async is for large payloads or long processing times. Read the scenario carefully for clues about response time, payload size, and traffic patterns.

The eighth trap is the underfitting fix. When a model is underfitting, the wrong fix is to add regularization, which is for overfitting. The right fix is to make the model more capable, with a more complex architecture, more features, longer training, or less regularization.

The ninth trap is the bias misdirection. When a question describes a model producing biased or unfair predictions, the first answer is usually not to tune the model. The first answer is to investigate and fix the data, or to use SageMaker Clarify to detect and analyze the bias.

The tenth trap is JumpStart versus Bedrock. Both involve foundation models, but they serve different purposes. JumpStart is part of SageMaker and gives you pretrained models you can fine-tune and deploy on your own infrastructure. Bedrock is a fully managed API for foundation models with no infrastructure on your part. If the question emphasizes managed API access to foundation models, the answer is Bedrock. If it emphasizes fine-tuning and deploying models with full control inside SageMaker, the answer is JumpStart.

The first question. A bank is building a model to flag suspicious credit card transactions, where roughly one in a thousand transactions is actually fraudulent. Which evaluation metric should the team prioritize, and why is accuracy a poor choice? The right answer is recall or F1, because the data is severely imbalanced and missing real fraud is much costlier than a few false alarms. Accuracy is misleading because a model that always predicts legitimate would score ninety-nine point nine percent.

The second question. A retailer has fifty million customer records and wants to discover natural shopping segments without specifying the segments in advance. What type of machine learning is this, and what is the problem type? It is unsupervised learning, because there are no labels, and the problem type is clustering, because the goal is to find natural groupings.

The third question. A model achieves ninety-eight percent training accuracy and seventy-one percent validation accuracy. What is the diagnosis, and name three reasonable fixes. The diagnosis is overfitting, indicated by the large gap between training and validation accuracy. Three fixes from a longer list could be collecting more training data, applying regularization, using dropout, simplifying the model, or applying early stopping.

The fourth question. A company wants to extract line items, totals, and tax amounts from PDF invoices. Which AWS service is correct? Amazon Textract, because it handles document data extraction including tables, forms, and key-value pairs.

The fifth question. A team needs to detect when the input data feeding a deployed model has shifted away from the data the model was trained on. Which SageMaker feature handles this? SageMaker Model Monitor, which is purpose-built for data drift detection.

The sixth question. A model needs to score fifty million customer records every Sunday night, with no real-time requirement. Which SageMaker deployment type fits? Batch Transform, because the workload is large, offline, and not latency-sensitive.

The seventh question. A startup is building a flight booking chatbot that needs to handle conversations involving departure city, destination, date, and number of passengers. Which AWS service is most appropriate? Amazon Lex, because it builds conversational interfaces using intents and slots.

The eighth question. Explain the difference between a parameter and a hyperparameter, and give one example of each. A parameter is learned by the model during training, such as the weights in a neural network. A hyperparameter is set by you before training, such as the learning rate.

The ninth question. Which deep learning architecture powers modern large language models, and why does it work so well? Transformers, because their attention mechanism allows every token in a sequence to directly look at every other token, enabling them to capture long-range dependencies and to scale efficiently through parallel training.

The tenth question. When would you choose Amazon Bedrock instead of Amazon SageMaker for a project? Choose Bedrock when you want a managed API for foundation models with no infrastructure overhead, particularly for generative AI applications. Choose SageMaker when you need to train a custom model on your own data, fine-tune extensively, or maintain full control over the machine learning lifecycle.
